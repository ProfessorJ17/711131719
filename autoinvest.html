1. Fibonacci Retracement: The Ultimate Support/Resistance Secret üîë

Fibonacci retracement is the go-to tool for traders looking to predict key price levels with astonishing accuracy. Based on the Fibonacci sequence and the Golden Ratio (1.618), these retracement levels are widely used by professional traders to identify support and resistance zones.

Key Fibonacci Levels

  • 0% ‚Äì Start of the trend
  • 23.6% ‚Äì Minor retracement (weak support)
  • 38.2% ‚Äì Moderate retracement (good support)
  • 50% ‚Äì Psychological level (critical spot!)
  • 61.8% ‚Äì The Golden Ratio (strongest support/resistance)
  • 78.6% ‚Äì Deep retracement (important turning point)
  • 100% ‚Äì Full reversal (watch closely!)

Formula to Calculate Fibonacci Levels:

Fibonacci retracement = High ‚Äì (Retracement Percentage * (High ‚Äì Low))

For example, in an uptrend from $100 (Low) to $200 (High), the 61.8% retracement level would be:

Retracement Level = 200 - (0.618 * (200 - 100))
Retracement Level = 200 - (0.618 * 100)
Retracement Level = 200 - 61.8
Retracement Level = 138.2

$138.2 is a key support level, and traders expect it to act as a critical bounce point if the price starts pulling back.

How to Use Fibonacci Retracement

  • Trend Continuation: If the price bounces from Fibonacci levels like 38.2%, 50%, or 61.8%, this suggests the trend will continue.
  • Trend Reversal: If the price breaks below 61.8%, it may indicate a reversal, signaling a potential market shift.

2. Gann's Square: Unlocking the Power of Time & Price ‚è≥üí°

Gann's Square is a mathematical marvel. It integrates time and price, believing they follow geometric patterns. W.D. Gann's approach shows us that markets follow predictable cycles and angles that indicate turning points in the market.

Key Gann Angles:

  • 1x1 (45¬∞ angle): Strongest trendline, key for bullish trends.
  • 1x2 (26.5¬∞ angle): Suggests moderate bullish momentum.
  • 2x1 (63.75¬∞ angle): Indicates a strong uptrend.
  • 1x8 (82.5¬∞ angle): Extreme movement; often unsustainable, signaling potential overbought conditions.

Formula for Gann Angles

Price Target = Initial Price ¬± (Time Units * Price Increment per Time Unit)

For example, if a stock is at $100 with a 1x1 angle ($1 per period), and the price moves for 10 periods:

Price Target = 100 + (10 * $1) = 110

If price stays above the 1x1 angle, we're in bullish territory, but if it breaks below, bearish signals emerge.

Key Additional Factors to Know

A. Market Structure & Trends

Identify the Trend:

  • Bullish Trend: Higher highs & higher lows.
  • Bearish Trend: Lower highs & lower lows.
  • Consolidation: Flat highs and lows or tight range-bound movement.

Trend Strength Confirmation:

  • Moving Averages: 50-day and 200-day moving averages are key to verify trend direction and strength.
  • ADX (Average Directional Index): Use ADX > 25 to confirm a strong trend; below 20 indicates weak or non-existent trend.

Breakout & Breakdown Signals:

  • Strong Breakout: Price closes above resistance with high volume.
  • Breakdown: Price closes below support with confirmation from momentum indicators (RSI, MACD).

Pattern Recognition:

  • Chart Patterns: Recognize and leverage patterns like head and shoulders, double tops/bottoms, and triangles for trend reversal or continuation signals.
  • Candlestick Patterns: Doji, engulfing, hammer, and shooting star patterns provide additional entry/exit insights.

B. Volume Confirmation

Volume as Validation:

  • High Volume on Breakout: Strong confirmation of trend continuation.
  • Low Volume on Breakout: Indicates potential fakeout. Ensure breakout sustains with follow-through volume.

Volume Divergence:

  • Bullish Divergence: Rising price, falling volume = potential reversal (weak trend).
  • Bearish Divergence: Falling price, rising volume = continuation of bearish momentum.

C. Market Cycles & Time Analysis

Gann's Time Cycles:

  • Focus on Gann‚Äôs key cycles (90, 144, 180, 360 days), but also watch for non-standard cycles tied to earnings or news events.

Elliott Wave Theory:

  • Incorporate Wave Counts to anticipate where the market might be in the overall cycle.
  • Wave 3 & 5 tend to be the most impulsive and profitable moves.
  • Watch for corrective waves (2 & 4) to adjust risk exposure.

Economic Cycles:

  • Economic Indicators: Macroeconomic data like CPI, PPI, GDP, and unemployment figures can guide market sentiment.
  • Interest Rates: Central bank policies (Fed, ECB, etc.) can significantly affect asset prices, especially growth stocks.

Seasonality:

  • Certain sectors or stocks show predictable seasonal patterns, such as consumer discretionary spending around holidays or tech ramp-ups before product launches.

# Function to calculate Fibonacci levels
def fibonacci_levels(data):
    high = data['high'].max()
    low = data['low'].min()
    diff = high - low
    return {
        '23.6%': high - 0.236 * diff,
        '38.2%': high - 0.382 * diff,
        '50%': high - 0.5 * diff,
        '61.8%': high - 0.618 * diff
    }

# Calculate RSI and MACD for momentum confirmation
def calculate_indicators(data):
    data['RSI'] = ta.RSI(data['close'], timeperiod=14)
    data['MACD'], data['MACD_signal'], _ = ta.MACD(data['close'])
    return data

# Define a function for position sizing
def calculate_position_size(account_balance, risk_percentage, stop_loss_distance):
    return (account_balance * risk_percentage) / stop_loss_distance

# Function to execute trades based on signals
def execute_trade(symbol, risk_per_trade, account_balance):
    data = exchange.fetch_ohlcv(symbol, timeframe='1h', limit=100)
    df = pd.DataFrame(data, columns=['timestamp', 'open', 'high', 'low', 'close', 'volume'])
    df = calculate_indicators(df)

    levels = fibonacci_levels(df)
    current_price = df['close'].iloc[-1]
    stop_loss_distance = current_price - levels['61.8%']

    if df['RSI'].iloc[-1] < 30 and df['MACD'].iloc[-1] > df['MACD_signal'].iloc[-1]:
        print(f"Buying {symbol} at {current_price}, Position Size: {position_size}")
        exchange.create_market_buy_order(symbol, position_size)

        print(f"Selling {symbol} at {current_price}")

from flask import Flask, render_template, request, redirect, url_for
import subprocess
import os

app = Flask(__name__)

# Define the command to start the bot
bot_command = "python /path/to/your/bot.py"

# Home route
@app.route('/')
def index():
    return render_template('index.html')

# Start the bot route
@app.route('/start', methods=['POST'])

    try:
        subprocess.Popen(bot_command, shell=True)
        return redirect(url_for('index'))
    except Exception as e:
        return f"Error starting bot: {e}"

# Stop the bot route (Assumes you know how to stop the bot via process management)
@app.route('/stop', methods=['POST'])
def stop_bot():
    os.system("pkill -f bot.py")  # Example to kill the process
    return redirect(url_for('index'))

if __name__ == "__main__":
    app.run(debug=True, host='0.0.0.0', port=5000)
